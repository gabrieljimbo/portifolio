# Default Review Standards

These are 18 universal copywriting rules and 12 red-flag triggers distilled from multiple rounds of senior creative director feedback on real projects. They apply to any marketing copy deck, regardless of industry.

**These defaults are always active.** Project-specific calibration (in `calibration.md`) adds on top. If calibration contradicts a default, calibration wins.

---

## Part 1: The 18 Rules

### Rule 1 -- Dual Benefit

Every major headline and benefit must connect both dimensions of the product's value proposition. What those two dimensions are is defined in your project's calibration file (e.g., lifestyle + financial, speed + reliability, creativity + efficiency). Copy that only hits one dimension is half-finished.

A senior creative director once said: "I want both -- keep the life I chose AND earn more."

---

### Rule 2 -- Factual Claims Must Be Defensible

Never write something a reader could immediately call out as untrue, even if it sounds good. If a claim requires context or caveats to be accurate, include them. "Same standard" is defensible. "Same apartment" in a different city is not.

---

### Rule 3 -- Hero Must Carry Hard Stats + Social Proof

The hero section must include 2-3 measurable statistics embedded in the layout (not just in a strip below), plus a social proof element. Stats must be based on immediate impact or outcomes, not vanity metrics.

---

### Rule 4 -- Quantitative Claims Need Anchoring

A claim like "save $4,000/month" floating without context lacks credibility. Every quantitative claim must specify the scenario, audience segment, or baseline it's calculated against. More logical, more to the point.

---

### Rule 5 -- Hero Detail Belongs Elsewhere

The hero must be sharp and high-level. Granular details, specific use cases, and deep explanations belong in scrollytelling or later sections. The hero states the claim; deeper sections unpack it.

---

### Rule 6 -- Product Scope Must Be Visually Showcased

Whatever defines the product's scope (cities, integrations, templates, markets, features) must be visually showcased -- not just mentioned in text. If the product's core value is geographic, show a map. If it's integrations, show the logos. If it's templates, show the grid. Telling without showing is a miss.

---

### Rule 7 -- Intro Section Between Hero and Benefits

There must be an intro/expansion section between the hero and the benefits. The hero states the claim; the intro unpacks it in a logical way. No repetition -- logical expansion only. Jumping from hero straight to benefits creates a gap in the emotional arc.

---

### Rule 8 -- Homepage Links to Depth Pages

The homepage is an overview. Each major section should link out to dedicated pages with deeper content. Internal linking creates a content system, not just a page.

---

### Rule 9 -- CTA Labels Are Commands, Never Questions

A question can precede a CTA to build context. But the CTA label itself -- the button text -- must be a direct command focused on the benefit. Never a question, never weak phrasing.

A senior creative director once said: "Internal CTAs cannot be a question! A question can be BEFORE a strong, to-the-point CTA LABEL."

---

### Rule 10 -- No Word Repetition (Unless Deliberate Rhetorical Device)

Lazy word repetition across nearby sentences is a junior mistake. Either vary the language, or intentionally escalate the repeated word into a rhetorical device with emotional payoff. Accidental repetition gets caught on the first draft at the senior level.

---

### Rule 11 -- Visual Direction Inline with Sections

Every section that needs a visual must include a description inline with the copy. Design direction: minimalist, clean lines, geometric shapes, no cheesy illustrations -- award-winning design standard. Include AI generation prompts for visual elements.

---

### Rule 12 -- Editorial Labels Throughout

The copy deck must include consistent editorial/design labels: eyebrows, metadata tags, component names, data labels. These help a designer build better layouts. "Frame" is not a label -- "eyebrow" is.

---

### Rule 13 -- Diagrams Inline, Not Appended

Diagram and visual specifications must live within their sections as callouts, not in a separate appendix. Track them with numbers. AI generation prompts can be collected at the end.

---

### Rule 14 -- Case Studies: Personal, Scannable, Linked

Case study headings must feel personal -- put the reader in the shoes of the user. On the homepage: scannable metrics only (measurable changes, impact, outcomes). Link to separate pages for full case studies.

---

### Rule 15 -- Distribute Testimonials as Editorial Quotes

Testimonials must not sit in one block. Distribute them across the page as editorial quotes, each matched to the section topic it supports. Make them logical and reflective of their section context.

---

### Rule 16 -- "How It Works in Practice" Sections

Include sections that show how the product works in real-world use -- not just abstract feature descriptions. Combine with testimonials where possible for credibility.

---

### Rule 17 -- SaaS/Product Essentials Checklist

A complete copy deck must include: final CTA section (pre-footer conversion block), pricing model, journal/blog section, main navigation, sitemap, and all secondary page copy. Missing any of these is a structural gap.

---

### Rule 18 -- The Overall Standard

A senior creative director once said: "You are on the good tracks but need to push it MUCH HARDER. This is senior-level work and you have to perform it on the highest level of marketing, copywriting, and UX design with the best psychological patterns in section sequence and building emotions to desire this product, being at the same time LOGICAL, to the point, and convincing without any buzzword, meaningless phrase, or cookie-cutter solution. PUSH IT HARDER."

This is the bar. Don't accept B+ work. Competent but not memorable is not done.

## Part 2: Red Flags -- Automatic REWRITE Triggers

If you encounter ANY of these, the section gets an automatic REWRITE verdict:

1. A sentence that carries zero specific information
2. A CTA phrased as a question
3. A CTA focused on what's wrong instead of what you gain
4. Any buzzword from the banned list (see below)
5. A benefit that is actually a feature (describes what the app does, not what changes for the user)
6. Copy framed around what the user is doing wrong (shame/loss framing)
7. A hero where you can't identify the product, audience, and benefit within 5 seconds
8. Developer-instruction language instead of finished web copy
9. A quantitative claim without an anchoring scenario or audience segment
10. A factually false claim (even if it sounds good)
11. Missing dual benefit in a major headline (only hits one value dimension)
12. Any word from the banned list used as a generic catch-all

---

## Part 3: Default Banned Words

These words are banned in all projects unless explicitly overridden in calibration:

**Generic catch-alls:** matters, where X happens, we're not just a...

**SaaS/AI buzzwords:** seamless, leverage, empower, unlock, revolutionize, game-changer, elevate, holistic, cutting-edge, innovative, robust, synergy, disruptive, next-generation, best-in-class

**AI writing patterns:** Additionally, delve, tapestry, landscape (abstract), testament, underscore, interplay, intricate/intricacies, fostering, garner, pivotal, vibrant, enduring, crucial

Also banned: any sentence that could apply to any product in any industry without modification.

Project-specific banned words can be added in calibration.

---

## Part 4: What "Pushing It Harder" Means

- Don't accept B+ work. Competent but not memorable is not done.
- Every section should have at least one line a creative director would highlight.
- The scroll experience must feel choreographed, not listed.
- The reader should want the product after reading the page.
- The numbers should make someone pull out a calculator because they seem too good (but they're real).
- No section should feel generated -- it should feel crafted by someone who deeply understands the target audience and designed every scroll moment to move them toward action.

When in doubt about whether copy is good enough, it isn't. Push harder.
